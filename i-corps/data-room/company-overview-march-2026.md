# Company Overview — March 2026

## What LYNA Is

LYNA is a hands-free voice wearable that delivers clinical intelligence to bedside nurses. It serves three functions: it is the delivery layer for clinical AI tools like ambient documentation and clinical decision support, it is equipped with institutional knowledge so nurses can access protocols, policies, and directories by voice, and it bridges communication by letting nurses call, page, and reach colleagues hands-free.

We build the hardware, the voice platform, and AI agents that connect hospital systems to the nurse by voice. For clinical AI tools that hospitals have already built or adopted, LYNA delivers them hands-free at the bedside. For hospital platforms that don't yet have AI, LYNA builds agents via API to make them voice-accessible. For the tools that do have AI — ambient documentation, clinical decision support, centralization platforms — LYNA is the device they run through to reach a nurse during care.

A nurse says "Hey LYNA, what's my patient's latest potassium and pull up the hypokalemia protocol." That's two systems, one voice command, and her hands never leave the patient. Nobody else has built this.

## Why This Matters Now

Hospitals are buying AI aggressively. Healthcare AI spending hit $1.4 billion in 2025, nearly tripling the prior year. Mayo Clinic is committing over $1 billion across 200+ AI projects. Kaiser deployed Abridge's ambient scribes across 40 hospitals. Epic launched AI Charting for bedside nursing in March 2026. Microsoft's Dragon Copilot is live for nurses at Tampa General. Voize raised $50 million for voice-based nursing documentation.

The money is flowing and the tools are being built, but they all terminate at a screen. Over 15 years of published research and our own 17 interviews across two hospital systems confirm the same pattern: when nurses need information during care, they ask a colleague instead of using the hospital's tools. Nurses have phones in their pockets and use them for mandatory tasks like medication scanning. But for the hospital's own clinical tools, a colleague is faster than navigating to the right app and hoping it returns the right answer.

Hospitals consolidated from multiple devices — badges, pagers, scanners — to smartphones specifically to reduce IT overhead. That consolidation saved IT costs but degraded the interaction model so severely that nurses stopped using discretionary tools during care. Stryker saw this. They acquired Vocera for $3.1 billion in 2022, already had a smartphone app (Vocera Edge, launched 2021), and still invested three years of R&D to launch the Sync Badge in 2025 — a new wearable badge for nurses — alongside the phone app. Their reasoning, stated directly: "Smartphones absolutely have a place, but hands-on care can sometimes make their use challenging." A $130 billion company with data from nearly 1,900 hospitals concluded that the phone alone is insufficient for bedside nursing. The Sync Badge does communication and pushed alerts. A nurse cannot ask it a question. LYNA's bet is that communication and alerts are baseline — the same form factor should also deliver clinical intelligence, institutional knowledge, and the AI tools the hospital is building.

Ambient AI for nursing faces an additional challenge: nurses work hands-on and silently, and ambient tools rely on spoken cues to generate documentation. A phone in a pocket is a poor form factor for capturing what nurses do during care. LYNA's always-on collar microphone is a fundamentally better capture device for ambient AI in nursing.

The intelligence is being built. The delivery mechanism to the nurse during care is not. LYNA is that delivery mechanism.

## Who We Serve

The user is the bedside nurse. The buyer is hospital leadership — the CIO, CNO, or CCIO who approved the technology investment and needs it to reach clinicians. Starting with nurses, expanding to all bedside clinicians.
