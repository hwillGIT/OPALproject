# Interview Scripts — Cluster 1: Staff/Bedside Nurses

## Introduction Script

> I'm doing Regional I-Corps at Mount Sinai — it's a research program where we investigate a problem and do interviews to test if it's real.
> The problem I'm looking at is how nurses access information and communicate during patient care, and how that breaks down at the bedside.
> I'm a nurse too, I work here, so I'm trying to understand how it actually works across different units and roles.
> You can decide if you want me to use your name or not for the research, but this is internal during the program.
> Do you mind if I record?

## Questions

### Opening / H1 — Is this problem universal?

1. "Tell me about the biggest challenges you face during a typical shift."
   - Redirect if needed: "Beyond staffing and scheduling, what about the actual work of taking care of patients — what slows you down?"
2. "How does that compare across the different units or hospitals you've worked in?"
3. "Is that something you've seen change as you've gained experience, or has it stayed consistent?"

### Core / H2 — Do clinicians bypass hospital systems at the bedside?

1. "When you need to know something during your shift — and you're not sure of the answer — what do you do?"
2. "When you can't find what you need quickly, what do you do?"
   - If they mention asking someone: "Walk me through the last time that happened."
3. "Think about the last time you needed information you didn't have while you were with a patient. What did you do?"
   - Follow up: "How long did that take from the moment you realized you needed it to when you had the answer?"
4. "Tell me about a time you needed help but couldn't get it right away. What happened?"
   - If they mention a phone or device change: "How does that compare to whatever you had before?"

### Core / H4 — Is the current device/interface failing bedside clinicians?

1. "Walk me through how you use your phone during a shift. What do you actually use it for?"
   - Follow up: "Is there anything you wish you could do on it that you can't — or that's too slow to bother with during patient care?"
2. "Has this hospital rolled out any new technology or apps in the last year or two — anything AI, any new clinical tools, anything on the intranet? Do you use any of it?"
   - If yes: "When do you use it? Can you use it while you're with a patient?"
   - If no: "Do you know if anything like that exists here?"
3. "Think about your last shift. Was there a moment where something took longer than it should have, or you had to work around the system to get something done?"

### Probe / H3 — Patient-specific guidance gap (light touch)

1. "Has there been a time where you weren't sure how to handle something with a specific patient and had to figure it out on the fly?"

### Closing

1. "You mentioned [specific thing they described]. If that got fixed, what would your shift look like?"

## Interview 1: David Hernandez — Float Pool RN

| Field | Details |
| --- | --- |
| Title / Role | Float Pool RN (8 years experience) |
| Organization | Mount Sinai Health System |
| Interview Type | In Person |
| Date | 2026-02-28 |
| Interviewer | Ruth |
| Cluster | Cluster 1 |
| Hypotheses Being Tested | H1, H2, H4 |

### IYE Summary

We hypothesized that the problem of bypassing hospital systems is universal across experience levels and unit types (H1), that clinicians need a different interface because current tools don't match bedside workflow (H2), and that the Vocera-to-phone transition created a regression in hands-free capability (H4).

He gets his assignment 5-10 minutes before a shift and takes about 15-20 minutes to feel settled on a new unit. He struggles with remembering access codes across units and sometimes doesn't have system access, requiring him to ask someone to get it. He described some units as territorial: "this is my computer, this is my that." When he needs to look up a protocol, he goes to a senior nurse or the charge nurse first because he already knows who is senior on most units. His second option is the intranet but he said navigating the computer would probably take longer. He estimated about five minutes to get an answer from a person. He calls other departments about once or twice per shift, roughly 10 minutes total. Float pool members share resources through a separate group chat, not Epic, and the float pool educator provides PDFs on topics nurses aren't comfortable with. When his hands are full and he needs help, he described the phones as "not the easiest to use" and defaults to pressing the call bell in the room and asking whoever responds. He could not recall a specific instance where a protocol didn't apply or a critical lab was inconvenient. When asked what would improve his workflow, he said "get here early" and had no suggestions for system-level changes.

**H1 partially validated:** problem consistent across units but he has adapted through experience. **H2 validated:** bypasses hospital systems entirely, defaults to asking a person. **H4 validated:** phones described as difficult, falls back to call bells when hands are full.

## Interview 2: Anonymous — ICU Travel Nurse

| Organization | Mount Sinai Health System |
| Interview Type | In Person |
| Date | 2026-03-03 |
| Interviewer | Ruth |
| Cluster | Cluster 1 |
| Hypotheses Being Tested | H1, H2, H4 |

### IYE Summary

She described her job as fairly straightforward and said she doesn't run into issues often. Her primary challenge is learning Mount Sinai's policies, which she described as more policy-driven than other hospitals she has worked at. She uses Policy Tech but said it often doesn't answer her question, so she ends up hunting for people to find answers. When she needs information, she asks a nearby nurse or the charge nurse first. She does not use the phone, doesn't like having it in her pocket, and doesn't find it helpful. She said the ICU beds are close enough that she can just talk to the person next to her. She suggested a group chat feature where nurses could message everyone on the unit at once, especially useful from isolation rooms when you need someone to grab something. She hated Vocera, didn't like wearing something heavy, and found it annoying to be interrupted in patient rooms. She acknowledged broadcast would have been useful on longer med-surg hallways but said the ICU layout doesn't require it. She was unaware of AI tools like the AI note taker on other units. When asked if there was a communication or navigation problem she wished could be solved, she said no, things run pretty smoothly here.

**H1 not validated in this context:** ICU layout, close proximity, high experience, and strong teamwork reduce the problem significantly. **H2 partially validated:** she still bypasses systems and asks people first, but doesn't experience it as a pain point. **H4 not validated:** she preferred losing Vocera and doesn't miss it.
